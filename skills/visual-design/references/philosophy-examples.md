# Design Philosophy Examples

Named aesthetic movements for visual design work. Use these as starting points —
name your movement, define its laws, then execute with full commitment.

---

## Swiss / International Style
**Movement name**: "Systematic Clarity"
**Philosophy**: Communication through grid, hierarchy, and typographic precision.
Color is functional — one dominant, one accent. Composition is structured but never
rigid. Every element earns its position by serving legibility and structure. The work
looks effortless because the system behind it is meticulously engineered.
**Visual laws**: Baseline grid. Flush-left type. Helvetica Neue or Aktiv Grotesk.
Red, black, white. Photography is documentary, never decorative.
**Forbidden**: Centered layouts. Decorative elements. More than 2 typefaces.

---

## Editorial Magazine
**Movement name**: "Controlled Tension"
**Philosophy**: The page is a stage. Scale contrast is the primary tool — enormous
type next to small details, vast empty space next to dense imagery. The composition
leads the eye on a journey, not a straight line. Typography is expressive, not merely
functional. Images breathe.
**Visual laws**: Asymmetric layouts. One dominant typographic gesture per spread.
Photography carries narrative weight. Color is restrained — usually 2 colors + black.
**Forbidden**: Symmetry. Equal-weight elements. Captions longer than one line.

---

## Japanese Minimalism
**Movement name**: "Chromatic Silence"
**Philosophy**: The space around the object is as important as the object itself.
A single color accent against white or off-white. Fine lines. The composition suggests
rather than states. Meaning arrives slowly, through sustained attention. Every element
that does not contribute to the essential idea is removed.
**Visual laws**: 90% negative space. One accent color. Thin-weight typography, small.
No more than 3 elements on the canvas. Paper texture encouraged.
**Forbidden**: Multiple colors. Large type. Busyness of any kind.

---

## Brutalism
**Movement name**: "Raw Architecture"
**Philosophy**: Truth through exposure. No decoration. No refinement. The structure
IS the aesthetic. Heavy typography. Overlapping elements that refuse to be polished.
System fonts or grotesques used at extreme weights. High contrast — near-black and
pure white with one jarring accent.
**Visual laws**: Grid used and deliberately broken. Type at extreme sizes.
Borders, rules, and structural lines are decorative. Color is used for aggression.
**Forbidden**: Elegance. Subtle anything. Drop shadows. Rounded corners.

---

## Art Deco / Geometric
**Movement name**: "Symmetrical Opulence"
**Philosophy**: Geometry as luxury. Perfect symmetry. Gold, cream, deep navy or
forest green. Ornament that is structural — every decorative element also holds
the composition together. Typography is display-first: tall, thin, elegant.
The work has a ceremonial quality — it marks an occasion.
**Visual laws**: Radial or bilateral symmetry. Geometric borders and frames.
Gold or metallic as accent. Serif or stylized display typefaces only.
**Forbidden**: Asymmetry. Informal type. Saturated colors. Photography.

---

## Psychedelic / Retro
**Movement name**: "Chromatic Pressure"
**Philosophy**: Maximum sensory input. Colors that vibrate against each other.
Forms that curve and flow. The composition is centrifugal — energy radiates from
the center. Hand-rendered quality, even if executed digitally. Nostalgia is a tool,
not a constraint.
**Visual laws**: Complementary color pairings. Curves and organic shapes.
Outlined type. Textures that suggest print. Visual density.
**Forbidden**: Clean lines. Corporate type. Negative space.

---

## Luxury / Refined
**Movement name**: "Quiet Authority"
**Philosophy**: Restraint is the signal of confidence. Muted, sophisticated palette —
stone, slate, warm cream, deep charcoal. Premium typography: a dignified serif
paired with a refined sans. Gold or copper as a single accent used sparingly.
The work does not announce itself. It expects you to come to it.
**Visual laws**: Generous negative space. Maximum 3 colors. Small type, carefully
spaced. No decorative elements that don't serve the composition.
**Forbidden**: Brightness. Multiple accent colors. Anything that could be called "fun".

---

## How to Write a New Philosophy

1. **Name it** (1–2 words): Should evoke a tension or a paradox
2. **State the core tension**: What two opposing forces does this movement hold?
3. **Visual laws** (3–5 rules): What must be true of any work in this movement?
4. **Forbidden** (2–3 items): What would immediately break the aesthetic?
5. **Reference** (optional): What era, discipline, or movement does it draw from?
